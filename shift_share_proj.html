Shift Share

Joe Yuke

12/31/2020

Shift-Share analysis is a technique used by urban planners, regional analysts, and economic developers to identify the sources of regional economic growth. Assuming job growth in Santa Barbara County can be explained by three components: National Share, Industry Mix, and Regional Shift, we can compare different sectors of the economy and identify competitive industries as the ones that outperform their counterpart at the state/national level. This information can then be used to inform public investment decisions and economic development policy.

In 2019, Santa Barbara County added 4,249 jobs, achieving a 2.5% increase across all industries. This is a significant change from the prior years growth of 2,715 jobs. Notably, employment success was driven by the Professional, Scientific & Technical Services sector which grew by 633 jobs; the Health Care & Social Assistance sectors gain of 647 jobs; and Agricultures increase of 1,227 jobs.

At the state and national levels, there were also increases in the total number of jobs but at less pronounced rates. California added 251,568 jobs in 2019, representing a 1.7% increase from the previous year, and the US added 1,806,905 jobs, growing by 1.5%.

Agriculture, Health Care, and the Accommodation & Food sector have consistently been Santa Barbara Countys largest industries in terms of employment. Over the past 10 years, all three have experienced fairly steady growth as well. However, to assess industry performance, we should look beyond the raw employment numbers.

Shift-Share analysis can explain how much of the job growth within an industry should be attributed to local conditions. As an example, Retail Trade, the countys fourth largest industry, added a mere 2 jobs in 2019. In fact, this small increase is a competitive performance compared to the national and state retail industries. The National Share calculation yields 18,816 which represents the number of jobs that would exist in the local retail industry had it grown at the same rate as the whole US economy. In other words, how much job growth should have been expected according to the national business cycle. Industry Mix assumes changes in employment consistent with the growth of the national retail industry. The US-SB analysis identifies a negative Industry Mix of -490. This is because the Retail Trade sector performed poorly at the national level, declining by 1.2% in 2019. Finally, the Regional Shift identifies leading industries by finding the differential between the local and national rates of growth and using it as a multiplier for the previous years job numbers. The Retail Trade sector for Santa Barbara County achieved a regional shift of 223 jobs, which represents 1.2% of local retail jobs. Industries that have positive Regional Shift scores are deemed competitive according to Shift-Share analysis. Adding all three calculations together yields the true employment numbers for the industry of interest in 2019. For this report, we also run the analysis substituting Californias employment numbers for the USs statistics. Interestingly, Santa Barbaras Retail industry performed even stronger relative to the state of California.

The Professional, Scientific, and Technical Services industry, which encompasses lawyers, engineers, researchers etc., achieved a relatively high Regional Shift of 331, or 2.8% of employment in that industry. This sector has been increasing slightly since 2016 but has largely remained consistent for the past 10 years.

Out of the Countys top three industries, Agriculture was the only one to realize a positive Regional Shift across both US and CA calculations. In the US-SB calculation, the Regional Shift was 1,236, or 5.1% of agricultural employment, making it the most competitive industry for Santa Barbara in 2019. Statewide and nationally, Agriculture represents a much smaller portion of the labor force and is typically outpaced in terms of growth when compared to Santa Barbara county. Health Care saw a modest increase of 647 jobs, but relative to the changes in Californias Health Care employment, this does not represent significant growth. Finally, the Accommodation and Food Services industry added only 222 jobs and underperformed compared to the national and particularly the state level. This is indicated in the much more negative Regional Shift for the CA-SB analysis.
